Dataset Name:
geotechnical_soil_behavior_classification_dataset

Problem Statement:
This synthetic dataset is designed for teaching beginner civil engineering graduate students how to apply multiclass classification machine learning methods to a realistic geotechnical engineering problem. The task is to predict a broad soil behavior class from commonly measured laboratory and field parameters.

Chosen Classification Problem:
Broad soil behavior classification inspired by standard geotechnical practice and the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS), aggregated into five beginner-friendly classes.

Target Variable:
Soil Behavior Class

Target Variable Definition:
Soil Behavior Class is a five-class categorical variable representing the dominant engineering behavior of the soil based on gradation and plasticity indicators.

Five Classes:
1. Gravelly Soil
   Description: Coarse-grained soil dominated by gravel-sized particles with low fines content and low plasticity.
   Broad engineering basis: Less than 50% passing the No. 200 sieve and less than 50% passing the No. 4 sieve.

2. Sandy Soil
   Description: Coarse-grained soil dominated by sand-sized particles, typically with low to moderate fines and low plasticity.
   Broad engineering basis: Less than 50% passing the No. 200 sieve and 50% or more passing the No. 4 sieve.

3. Silty Soil
   Description: Fine-grained soil with silt-like behavior, generally plotting below or near the A-line on the plasticity chart.
   Broad engineering basis: 50% or more passing the No. 200 sieve, with plasticity index below the clay-dominant range.

4. Low-Plasticity Clay
   Description: Fine-grained clayey soil with liquid limit below 50 and plasticity index above the A-line.
   Broad engineering basis: 50% or more passing the No. 200 sieve, clay-like plasticity, LL < 50.

5. High-Plasticity Clay
   Description: Fine-grained clayey soil with high liquid limit and high plasticity.
   Broad engineering basis: 50% or more passing the No. 200 sieve, clay-like plasticity, LL >= 50.

Class Distribution in This Dataset:
- Gravelly Soil: 180 rows
- Sandy Soil: 250 rows
- Silty Soil: 210 rows
- Low-Plasticity Clay: 220 rows
- High-Plasticity Clay: 140 rows

Input Features:
1. Natural Moisture Content (%)
   Description: Gravimetric water content of the in situ sample.
   Unit: %
   Expected Range: about 2 to 70

2. Bulk Unit Weight (kN/m^3)
   Description: Total or moist unit weight of the soil sample.
   Unit: kN/m^3
   Expected Range: about 14 to 23

3. Dry Unit Weight (kN/m^3)
   Description: Dry unit weight of the soil sample.
   Unit: kN/m^3
   Expected Range: about 11 to 22

4. Specific Gravity of Solids (-)
   Description: Specific gravity of the soil solids.
   Unit: dimensionless
   Expected Range: about 2.62 to 2.80

5. Percent Passing No. 4 Sieve (%)
   Description: Percentage of soil finer than the No. 4 sieve (4.75 mm).
   Unit: %
   Expected Range: about 0 to 100

6. Percent Passing No. 40 Sieve (%)
   Description: Percentage of soil finer than the No. 40 sieve (0.425 mm).
   Unit: %
   Expected Range: about 0 to 100

7. Percent Passing No. 200 Sieve (%)
   Description: Percentage of soil finer than the No. 200 sieve (0.075 mm), commonly used to distinguish coarse-grained and fine-grained soils.
   Unit: %
   Expected Range: about 0 to 100

8. Median Particle Size D50 (mm)
   Description: Particle size at which 50% of the sample is finer.
   Unit: mm
   Expected Range: about 0.001 to 30

9. Coefficient of Uniformity Cu (-)
   Description: Gradation descriptor derived from particle-size distribution.
   Unit: dimensionless
   Expected Range: about 2 to 20

10. Liquid Limit (%)
    Description: Water content at the liquid limit from Atterberg limit testing.
    Unit: %
    Expected Range: about 15 to 95

11. Plasticity Index (%)
    Description: Difference between liquid limit and plastic limit, representing the plastic range.
    Unit: %
    Expected Range: about 0 to 65

12. Standard Penetration Test N60 (blows/0.3 m)
    Description: Corrected SPT blow count used as an index of relative density or consistency.
    Unit: blows per 0.3 m
    Expected Range: about 1 to 60

How the Synthetic Data Was Constructed:
- The dataset was generated from engineering-informed latent variables rather than arbitrary random numbers.
- Particle-size behavior was represented using a lognormal-style gradation concept with class-dependent D50 and Cu values, then converted into realistic sieve passing percentages.
- Fine-grained classes were differentiated using liquid limit and plasticity index in a broad USCS-inspired manner, including the A-line concept and the liquid limit threshold at 50.
- Dry unit weight, bulk unit weight, and natural moisture content were generated to be internally consistent with standard soil phase relationships.
- Specific gravity was kept within realistic ranges for common inorganic soils.
- Standard penetration resistance was generated to vary with density/void ratio, water content, particle size, and plasticity so that coarse dense soils tend to have higher N60 values than soft, wet, highly plastic clays.
- Random variability was intentionally included so that the classes overlap moderately and the machine learning task is educational rather than trivial.

Engineering Logic Behind Class Assignment:
The target class was assigned using broad engineering rules inspired by standard soil classification practice:
- Coarse-grained versus fine-grained behavior was primarily controlled by percent passing the No. 200 sieve.
- Gravelly versus sandy behavior was primarily controlled by percent passing the No. 4 sieve within coarse-grained soils.
- Silt-like versus clay-like behavior in fine-grained soils was controlled by liquid limit and plasticity index relative to the plasticity chart A-line.
- High-plasticity clay was identified by clay-like plasticity combined with liquid limit at or above 50.
These rules produce classes that are realistic, interpretable, and suitable for educational classification exercises.

Missing Data Strategy:
- Every input feature column contains exactly 70 missing values out of 1000 rows, which is 7% missingness per feature.
- Missingness was inserted independently and randomly by column.
- Missing values are represented as NaN.
- The target column contains no missing values.

Intended Educational Use:
This dataset is intended for beginner machine learning exercises in geotechnical engineering, including:
- handling missing data and imputation
- exploratory data analysis
- feature scaling and preprocessing
- correlation review and engineering interpretation
- training multiclass classifiers
- comparing algorithms such as logistic regression, decision trees, random forests, and gradient boosting
- model evaluation using confusion matrices, precision, recall, F1-score, and feature importance

Assumptions:
- The classes are broad engineering behavior groups, not exact ASTM D2487 group symbols.
- Organic soils, peat, collapsible soils, and expansive soil mineralogy effects were excluded to keep the problem beginner-friendly.
- All soils are treated as inorganic mineral soils for this educational dataset.
- Some laboratory measurements that may be omitted in practice for very clean coarse soils were still given numeric values here to keep the tabular dataset suitable for machine learning.
- Values were rounded to practical reporting precision.
